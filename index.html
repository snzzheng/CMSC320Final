Analysis of Cars: How does engine size, number of cylinders, and vehicle curb weight affect horsepower, mpg, and cost?¶

Introduction:¶

It has been common knowledge amongst car enthusiasts and professionals within the automotive industry that specifications of a car engine (such as its size and the number of cylinders it has) affects its performance. However, as modern automobiles are forced by regulations and emissions to size down and implement complicated technologies such as auto start-stop ignition and cylinder deactivation, the ability to clearly define how engine size and cylinders affects performance has become more muddied. This is especially the case when eight, ten, and twelve cylinder engines are forced to slowly die out of production cars as a result of increasingly restrictive automotive regulations. These type of engines are left to only be placed in high-end exotic sports cars. In fact, within the next few decades, we many no longer see pure gas powered cars and all cars may be forced to adopt some sort of hybrid drivetrain. As a result, I have looked to this 1985 dataset from Ward's Automotive Yearbook titled "Model Import Car and Truck Specifications." With this dataset, I expect to more accurately examine how the specifications of an engine (without 21st century technologies) affects its performance and efficiency.

Part 2: Data Management/Representation¶

Tidying and Simplifying the Data:¶

Removing Unnecessary Columns¶

We will remove the symboling and normalized-losses columns as this does not relate to the analysis that we are focusing on.

We will remove the num-of-doors, body-style, drive-wheels, engine-location columns as they are not significantly relevant to our analysis and we have other columns that either overlap with these columns or are more representative of the data we want.

We will remove the wheel-base, length, width, and height columns as curb-weight is much more relevant than the dimensions of the car.

We will remove the engine-type, fuel-system, bore, stroke, compression-ratio, and peak-rpm columns as these are very technical specifications that do not contribute significantly to our general analysis.

Combining City MPG and Highway MPG Into One Value¶

I will also simplify our city and highway mpg statistics by combining them into a single combine mpg value. Combined mpg is often calculated for production vehicles as a way to represent the expect miles per gallon a driver can expect from a vehicle based on typical driving patterns. Combined mpg is calculated by weighting the city value by 55% and the highway value by 45%. I can then remove the city-mpg and highway-mpg columns.

In [31]:
#computing the combined mpg of each vehicle and removing the city and highway mpg columns
df['combined-mpg'] = (df['city-mpg'] * 0.55) + (df['highway-mpg'] * 0.45)
df.drop(['city-mpg','highway-mpg'], axis=1, inplace=True)

Part 3: Exploratory Data Analysis¶

Data Visualization and Analysis:¶

After removing unnecessary attributes and missing data, as well as tidying our dataset, we now have a dataframe that consists only of the following columns:

  • curb-weight
  • num-of-cylinders
  • engine-size
  • horsepower
  • combined-mpg
  • price

In my dataframe, I effectively have two different types of columns/attributes. Some of the columns are indepenent varables while others are dependent variables. The independent columns are curb-weight, num-of-cylinders, and engine-size. The dependent columns are horsepower, combined-mpg, and price. However, comparing some of the indepedent variables against certain dependent variables will not make sense. For example, it does not make sense to analyze the correlation between curb-weight and horsepower. I will analyze all combinations of the attributes that do make sense.

After plotting the curb weights against price and combined mpg for each vehicle in the dataset, we can see a rough relationship for both.

For weight versus price, we can see a rough positive linear relationship. As the weight of a vehicle increases, so does the price as well. The data is more clustered and tight on the left-hand side (as vehicles from the 1980's were often not very heavy) and becomes more spread out as we move towards the right-hand side.

For weight versus combined mpg, we can see a decently well defined negative linear relationship. As the weight of a vehicle increases, the combined mpg tends to decrease. The data is more clustered in the middle-left of the chart, but in general, the data has a decently tight clustering to it and we can easily define a linear pattern through the center of the data.
